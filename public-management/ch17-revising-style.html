The Four Principles
1
Characters as Subjects

Every story has characters ‚Äî the people, groups, or things the story is about. In clear writing, those characters should appear as the grammatical subjects of your sentences.

The problem in academic writing is that real characters get buried inside prepositional phrases or turned into modifiers, while abstract nouns like "the reason," "the presence," or "the nature of" occupy the subject slot. The reader is left asking: who is actually doing something here?

Subjects should be short, specific, and concrete. If your subject is longer than 6-7 words, it's probably too abstract.

‚ùå Characters buried
"The reason for Locke's frequent repetition lies in his distrust of the naming power of words."
‚úÖ Characters as subjects
"Locke frequently repeated himself because he did not trust the power of words to name things accurately."

Notice how the second version has Locke and he as subjects ‚Äî the actual doer. The first version has "the reason" as the subject, which doesn't do anything.

2
Actions as Verbs (Not Nouns)

When you take a strong verb and turn it into a noun, that's called a nominalization: decide ‚Üí decision, resist ‚Üí resistance, fail ‚Üí failure, measure ‚Üí measurement.

The problem? Once you've turned the action into a noun, you need a vague replacement verb like make, have, do, provide, ensure to carry the sentence. The real action disappears, and so does the character doing it.

‚ùå Actions hidden in nouns
"Our standardization of an index for the measurement of thought disorders has made possible the quantification of response."
‚úÖ Actions as verbs
"Now that we have standardized an index to measure thought disorders, we can quantify how patients respond."

Count the nominalizations in the first version: standardization, measurement, quantification. In the second, those are verbs: standardized, measure, quantify. The sentence becomes shorter, clearer, and more direct.

‚ö° Nuance: Don't mechanically convert every abstract noun into a verb. If the abstraction is your central subject (e.g., in philosophy or theory), keep it. The rule is: don't surround one necessary abstraction with unnecessary additional ones.
3
Old Before New

Start each sentence with familiar information ‚Äî something already mentioned, or easily inferred from context. Save new, complex, or surprising information for the end.

This is what creates the feeling of "flow" in writing. When a sentence opens with something the reader recognizes, they have a stable anchor to process whatever comes next. When it opens with something unfamiliar, they feel lost before the sentence even gets going.

Think of it as a bridge: each sentence should start by connecting to what came before, then carry the reader to something new.

‚ùå New information first (disorienting)
"A pattern of explosive demographic growth that exacerbates existing economic disparities characterizes many developing nations."
‚úÖ Old before new (flows naturally)
"Many developing nations are characterized by a pattern of explosive demographic growth that exacerbates existing economic disparities."
‚ö° Priority rule: If you ever have to choose between this principle and Principle 1 (characters as subjects), this one wins. Flow matters more than having a character in the subject position.
4
Complexity Last

The end of a sentence carries the most emphasis ‚Äî it's what lingers in the reader's mind. Place your heaviest, most complex, or most important information there.

This matters in three specific situations:

‚ë† Introducing new technical terms
Let familiar context come first, then land the new term at the end so the reader absorbs the definition before the jargon.

‚ë° Introducing long, complex information
Keep the subject short and familiar (even using passive voice if needed), so the dense material arrives after the reader is oriented.

‚ë¢ Introducing what follows
End the first sentence of a paragraph with the key concept the rest of the paragraph will develop ‚Äî it acts as a signpost.

Active vs. Passive Voice

The standard advice "always use active voice" is too blunt. The real question isn't "is this active or passive?" ‚Äî it's "does this sentence begin with familiar information?"

Sometimes passive voice is the right choice because it lets you move familiar information to the front.

Critical rule: Don't apply these principles while drafting ‚Äî you'll freeze up and tie yourself in knots. Write freely first, then revise systematically.
