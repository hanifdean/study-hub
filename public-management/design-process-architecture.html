Chapter 1: What is Design?
Three Properties of Design
🔧 Design is a Service

Expert advice on how to approach a problem, plus a clear plan for solving it. In architecture: a spatial solution. In public management: an organizational solution.

🔄 Design is Iterative

Design doesn't happen once. It's a cyclical activity — the same process steps repeat at different levels of detail. Different outputs at different points, but strong similarities in the process.

🎨 Design is Personal

Successful designers develop their own process. Their work says something about their ideas and their way of seeing the world. The outcome is for others — but the process is yours.

The Architect as Coordinator

Modern buildings involve many different professionals — architects, structural engineers, mechanical engineers, project managers, contractors. Each produces different information that must be coordinated.

"The architect, along with others, must ensure that the design roles of these different groups are integrated into the overall process, so that the best possible ideas from each discipline are brought together. This means that there is a coordinating role, as well as a design role, for the architect."
— Makstutis, p.14
⚡ Public management parallel: Stanton's role in the Blackstone Project is exactly this dual role — she's both designing the pilot AND coordinating air, water, hazwaste, and OTA specialists into a coherent system. The BPS readings describe the problem-solver as expert or facilitator. Makstutis adds a third role: coordinator.
The Four Design Stages (Iterative Cycle)
1
Research
Understand the client, the site, the context. Look at precedents. "One of the primary methods of research for a designer is to look at what exists."
2
Proposition
Develop ideas into distinct proposals. Early propositions have little detail. Explore many potential solutions quickly — breadth before depth.
3
Analysis
Evaluate against criteria: performance, function, concept, aesthetics. Crits and reviews from others. Client feedback is crucial. Analysis is "productive as well as reflective."
4
Revision
If unworkable → new proposition. If promising → fine-tune. New information can reopen earlier decisions. Loops back to research, proposition, or analysis.

This cycle repeats at every scale — from the initial concept to the smallest detail. Iteration is not a weakness — it's how design works.

Chapter 8: Developing Your Design Process
1. Expand Your World View

Constantly look for things that open new ways of thinking. "Why was it designed this way? How could it have been done differently?"

2. Reflective Practice

Actively review your own work and process. Not self-criticism — constructive self-analysis that helps you improve and think more clearly.

3. Build an Arsenal of Approaches

Every project is different. You need multiple starting points, exploration methods, and communication techniques. Combine, experiment, test yourself.

"Your design process should be based on your ideas and your views. You may look to the work and process of others, as a way of exploring, but in the end you must find your own methods."
— Makstutis, p.185

What Makstutis Adds That BPS Misses
🔑 The Coordination Role

BPS frames the problem-solver as expert (content) or facilitator (process). Makstutis adds a third: coordinator — integrating many specialists into a coherent whole. This is arguably the most important role in public management, where programs always span multiple agencies and disciplines.

🔑 Precedents as Design Source, Not Just Evaluation

BPS uses case-based reasoning to evaluate designs. Makstutis says precedents are also a primary source of design ideas — you look at what exists to understand how problems have already been solved. Dual use: inspiration AND evaluation.

🔑 Progressive Stages with Richer Vocabulary

BPS has "design part → change part → learning part." Makstutis gives five stages from concept to production — a much more granular vocabulary for understanding where you are in the process and what that stage demands.

💡 Key Takeaway

Design in architecture and design in public management share the same DNA: iterative, collaborative, precedent-informed, and never purely technical. The architect coordinates specialists toward a coherent building; the public manager coordinates agencies toward a coherent program. Both require creative leaps, progressive refinement from concept to detail, and balancing the designer's vision with the user's needs.
