Dual nature of artefacts, the science of the artificial, and why people can't draw bicycles

1 Kroes ‚Äî Dual Nature of Technical Artefacts

Kroes argues that technical artefacts have a dual nature ‚Äî they can be described in two fundamentally different ways that cannot be reduced to each other:

Structural Description

What the artefact is physically ‚Äî its materials, components, geometry, physical properties. Governed by laws of physics. Objective, measurable.

Functional Description

What the artefact does ‚Äî its purpose, use, the human intention behind it. Governed by intentional frameworks. Depends on context and user.

Two Frameworks

  • Physical framework: Describes the artefact using natural science ‚Äî causal mechanisms, material properties, physical processes
  • Intentional framework: Describes the artefact using purpose and design intent ‚Äî what it's for, what problem it solves

Designer vs User Intentions

There's a crucial distinction between designer intentions (what the creator meant the artefact to do) and user intentions (how people actually use it). These don't always align ‚Äî think of how people use screwdrivers as pry bars, or how social media platforms get used in ways their designers never intended.

Epistemological & Ontological Implications

Epistemologically: You need both descriptions to fully understand a technical artefact. Knowing only the physical structure doesn't tell you the function, and knowing only the function doesn't tell you how it works.

Ontologically: The artefact exists simultaneously as a physical object AND as a functional entity. Neither description is more "real" than the other ‚Äî the dual nature is fundamental.

2 Simon ‚Äî Understanding the Natural and Artificial Worlds

The Need for a "Science of the Artificial"

Simon argues that the natural sciences are insufficient for understanding designed systems ‚Äî things made by humans to serve purposes. We need a distinct science of the artificial that deals with how things ought to be (design) rather than just how things are (analysis).

Professional Schools' Crisis of Identity

Engineering, business, medicine, law ‚Äî professional schools struggled because they tried to become more "scientific" by importing natural science methods. But their core activity is design, not analysis. Simon argues they should reclaim design as their intellectual core.

Artefact as Interface

üîó Inner Environment ‚Üî Outer Environment

Every artefact sits at the interface between:

Inner environment: The internal structure and mechanisms ‚Äî how it works under the hood

Outer environment: The context in which it operates ‚Äî the problems it faces, the goals it serves

The artefact's behavior emerges from the interaction between inner and outer environments. You can understand what it does (outer) without understanding how (inner) ‚Äî this is functional equivalence.

Functional Equivalence

Different inner environments can produce the same outer behavior. A mechanical watch and a digital watch both tell time ‚Äî same function, completely different mechanisms. This means function doesn't determine structure (and vice versa).

State vs Process Descriptions

State description: What the system looks like at a point in time (snapshot). Process description: How the system evolves over time (movie). Both are needed ‚Äî state tells you where you are, process tells you how you get somewhere.

Simulation

Simon was an early advocate of simulation as a way of understanding. If you can simulate a system, you understand it. Simulation bridges the gap between inner mechanism and outer behavior.

Hierarchy & Near-Decomposability

Complex systems are almost always hierarchical ‚Äî composed of subsystems that are themselves composed of sub-subsystems. These systems are nearly decomposable: interactions within subsystems are much stronger than interactions between subsystems. This is what makes complexity manageable ‚Äî you can understand each level somewhat independently.

3 Lawson ‚Äî The Science of Cycology

The Illusion of Explanatory Depth

People systematically overestimate how well they understand how everyday things work. Lawson demonstrates this with a beautifully simple experiment: ask people to draw a bicycle.

üö≤ The Bicycle Drawing Experiment

When asked to draw a bicycle from memory, over 40% of participants make significant structural errors ‚Äî misplacing the chain, connecting the frame incorrectly, or putting pedals in the wrong position.

This is a bicycle. Something most people have ridden. Something they see every day. And they still can't accurately represent its structure.

Key Findings

  • Experts vs non-experts: People with mechanical expertise (engineers, bike mechanics) made significantly fewer errors, but even some experts got details wrong
  • Sex differences: Men tended to produce more accurate drawings than women, likely reflecting differences in mechanical exposure and interest rather than inherent ability
  • Self-rating disconnect: People who rated their understanding as high often produced drawings with major errors ‚Äî confidence and competence were poorly correlated

The takeaway: familiarity with function creates an illusion of understanding structure. You know what a bicycle does (gets you from A to B) without knowing how it actually works (chain drives rear wheel through gear mechanism, frame geometry creates stability, etc.).

üîó How They Connect

Kroes gives us the philosophical framework: artefacts have a dual nature (structural + functional), and you need both descriptions.

Simon gives us the practical science: artefacts sit at the interface between inner and outer environments, and we need design-oriented disciplines to understand them.

Lawson gives us the empirical evidence: people systematically lack functional understanding even for simple, everyday objects.

The thread connecting all three: people only engage with the functional side (Kroes) ‚Äî they understand the outer environment (Simon) ‚Äî without ever looking under the hood at the structural side (Kroes) or inner environment (Simon). Lawson proves this empirically.

üí¨ From the Discussion

"People understand the outer environment (what a bicycle is for) but not the inner environment (how gears, chain, and pedals connect). Simon's interface concept maps directly onto Kroes's structure-function distinction."

Kroes's structural description = Simon's inner environment. Kroes's functional description = Simon's outer environment. And Lawson shows that for most people, the inner/structural side is effectively invisible ‚Äî we navigate the world through function alone, confident we understand more than we do.

üí° Why This Matters for Public Management

Public institutions are artefacts too ‚Äî designed systems with inner mechanisms and outer purposes. Citizens (and often managers) engage only with the functional surface: what the institution does. Understanding how it works ‚Äî its inner environment, its structural mechanisms ‚Äî requires deliberate effort. And as Lawson shows, most people overestimate how well they already understand it.
