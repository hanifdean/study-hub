Chapter 2: Problem-Solving Projects in Organizations
The Regulative Cycle

BPS projects follow the Regulative Cycle (Van Strien, 1997) — five process steps:

1
Problem Definition
What's the problem? Whose problem?
2
Analysis & Diagnosis
What causes it? What are consequences?
3
Plan of Action
Solution design + change plan
4
Intervention
Implement the changes
5
Evaluation
Did it work? Learning & adaptation
Three Parts of a Full BPS Project
PartWhat HappensSteps
DesignRedesign the system + create change plan + build organizational supportSteps 1, 2, 3
ChangeImplement through changing roles, routines, toolsStep 4
LearningOrganization adapts; people relearn how to work effectivelyStep 5

Students typically leave after the design part — they have an effort commitment but not a result commitment (can't commit organizational resources).

Characteristics of Business Problems
⚠️ Business Problems Are Not Research Problems

Not given, but chosen — problems emerge from a "mess" of issues; stakeholders choose which to work on based on power, interest, and feasibility

Perception-based — stakeholders compare their perception of performance against norms and declare a gap

Open-ended — multiple good solutions exist (not one right answer)

Value-laden — charged with interests, power, politics

Satisficing — solved "good enough" within time/effort constraints

"Change muddle" — even sound designs face interference during implementation; improvisation always needed

⚡ Watch out for: Perception problems (gap based on inaccurate information — performance is actually fine) and Target problems (gap based on unattainable norms — the standard is unrealistic). Only work on real problems.
Chapter 3: Design-Focused Problem-Solving
Builds on Simon, But Differs in Two Key Ways
Difference 1: Not Purely Technical

BPS isn't just about a smart solution — it's about realizing actual performance improvement, which always involves organizational change. Following Tichy (1983), significant change must be managed across three subsystems simultaneously:

• Technical — the system/process itself
• Political — interests, power, stakeholders
• Cultural — values, perceptions, attitudes

Rational problem-solving only handles the technical. This methodology adds political and cultural.

Difference 2: Flexible Process, Not Rigid Phases

Classical approaches demand finishing each phase before starting the next. This methodology uses flexible process steps with:

• Iterations — jumping back to previous steps
• Explorations — jumping ahead to test ideas before analysis is complete

Example: Problem definition → first analysis → explore possible solutions → back to detailed analysis → back to solution design → maybe redefine the problem entirely

Project Deliverables
  1. Problem definition

The Core Design Loop: Synthesis-Evaluation Iterations
The Design Process
Problem Analysis → Develop Specifications →
⬇️
CREATIVE LEAP (Synthesis) → Proposed Solution →
⬇️
Evaluation: Does it solve the problem?
⬇️
YES → Done  |  NO → Back to Synthesis

The creative leap can't be prescribed — but you can manage its inputs: good problem analysis, clear specifications, knowledge of similar cases, and relevant literature/precedents.

🎯 Idealized Design (Ackoff)

Step 1: Design the ideal solution — technically, economically, socially viable — BUT ignoring all implementation constraints. Not science fiction, but not constrained by "how do we get from here to there."

Step 2: Then assess: how much of this ideal can actually be implemented given real constraints?

Normal design approaches from present → what can we change?
Idealized design approaches from ideal → what's possible?

💡 Key Takeaway

Solving real organizational problems requires a creative leap that analysis alone cannot produce — plus managing the technical, political, and cultural dimensions simultaneously. Even brilliant designs face the "change muddle." Design satisfices, iterates, and accepts that implementation will be messy — but a sound design still dramatically improves the odds.
