The unifying thread across all four cases: How do you achieve coordinated action when you have mandate without command? Each case reveals a different mechanism — information systems (Brazil), design cycles (Kumbh Mela), governance protocols (E-JUST) — and Blackstone must combine them.

Case C

E-JUST: Egypt-Japan University of Science & Technology

A bilateral development cooperation project to create a Japanese-style postgraduate technology university in Egypt — where detailed academic planning masked a governance vacuum.

R/D
The Record of Discussions
Bilateral agreement framework
  • Detailed on: Academic programs, curriculum structure, research areas, student intake targets
  • Vague on: Governance structures, decision-making authority, management protocols, collaboration mechanisms
  • This asymmetry became the root cause of subsequent dysfunction
T
Impossible Timeline
Less than 1 year from R/D to students
  • R/D signed late 2008
  • First students arriving September 2009
  • Under 12 months to build governance, recruit faculty, develop curriculum, prepare facilities
  • Timeline pressure made governance shortcuts feel necessary — but they became structural weaknesses
Core Tension

Prof. Khairy's centralized management style vs. the Japanese collaborative approach. The Egyptian side kept Japanese counterparts out of internal management decisions, undermining the bilateral nature of the project. One person became the bottleneck for all decision-making.

Key People

Prof. Khairy — E-JUST President (Egyptian side) Dr. Tsunoda — Japanese coordination lead Prof. Miki — Japanese academic advisor
What Went Wrong: Governance by Omission
  • No shared governance protocol: The R/D assumed goodwill would fill governance gaps — it didn't
  • Centralized bottleneck: Prof. Khairy controlled all decisions; Japanese side had no formal channel to influence internal management
  • Cultural mismatch amplified: Egyptian hierarchical tradition vs. Japanese consensus-building became unresolvable without structural frameworks
  • Bilateral in name only: Japanese expertise on governance/management was systematically excluded from where it was most needed
Lesson for Blackstone

Specify governance and management arrangements before launch. Don't let one person become the bottleneck. Design collaboration protocols explicitly — don't assume they'll emerge naturally from shared goals.

Cross-Reference

Brazil in Action

SPI's coordination of 42 priority infrastructure projects without formal authority over line ministries. Full study guide →

SPI
The Authority Paradox
Coordinating without commanding
  • SPI (Secretariat of Strategic Projects) sat in the presidency but had no formal authority over the ministries implementing the 42 projects
  • Ministries were sovereign in their domains — SPI couldn't direct them, fire their staff, or override their decisions
  • Challenge: How do you get 42 projects done on time when you can't tell anyone what to do?
Form of Functioning

"Effective central oversight WITHOUT formal hierarchical authority"

Mechanism 1: MIS

Management Information System that made project status transparent. Every ministry had to report progress on standardized metrics. This created visibility — and visibility created accountability. Nobody wants to be the one with red indicators in front of the presidency.

Mechanism 2: Conditional Fund Release

Budget tranches were tied to milestone completion. This wasn't punishment — it was structural alignment of incentives. Ministries had sovereignty over execution but resources flowed conditional on demonstrated progress.

Lesson for Blackstone

Build accountability through information systems, not hierarchy. Use conditional resource mechanisms to align incentives without requiring command authority. Make performance visible so that social and political pressure does the enforcement work.

Chapter 3 — DRC Extended Illustration

Kumbh Mela

Managing the world's largest temporary gathering through design cycles, with Herbert Simon's framework as analytical lens and Pahl & Beitz as the operational model.

F
Analytical Frameworks Applied
Simon + Fayol/POSDCORB + Pahl & Beitz
  • Herbert Simon: Decision-making as the core of management — but his framework alone is insufficient (see Simon Gaps section)
  • Fayol / POSDCORB: Classical management functions (Planning, Organizing, Staffing, Directing, Coordinating, Reporting, Budgeting) — provides vocabulary but misses design process
  • Pahl & Beitz (adapted): Engineering design methodology applied to public management — conceptual design, embodiment design, detailed design
Form of Functioning

"Timely resolution" — The Mela has an immovable deadline (religious calendar). Everything must work by a specific date. This forces a different management logic than open-ended programs.

Key People & Roles

Ashish Goel — Directing (Mela Officer) Saloni Goel — Programming

The distinction between directing and programming is critical. Directing = setting intentions and resolving conflicts at the top. Programming = translating those intentions into operational sequences that teams can execute. These are different functions requiring different skills, often done by different people.

Design Cycles

Conceptual Design
→
Embodiment Design
→
Detailed Design
  • Conceptual: What are we trying to achieve? What's the overall approach? (e.g., "create a temporary city for 30 million pilgrims")
  • Embodiment: What are the major subsystems and how do they relate? (e.g., water supply, sanitation, crowd management, road layout)
  • Detailed: Specific operational plans, schedules, resource allocations for each subsystem
  • These stages aren't strictly sequential — there are feedback loops between them, but the distinction matters for management
Lesson for Blackstone

Distinguish directing from programming. Use the pilot-as-test-cycle approach — treat the Blackstone pilot as an embodiment design that will generate feedback for detailed design. Timely resolution matters — set clear milestones and let the deadline discipline the process.

Synthesis Case

Massachusetts Blackstone Project

Stanton's challenge: designing a cross-media pollution prevention pilot at EPA Region 1 with policy authority but no line authority over the people who need to do the work.

S
Stanton's Situation
The authority gap
  • Policy authority: Stanton has been given a mandate to run a cross-media pollution prevention pilot in the Blackstone River Valley
  • No line authority: The regional office staff who need to implement the pilot don't report to Stanton
  • Cross-media challenge: Pollution prevention cuts across air, water, and waste divisions — each with their own culture, metrics, and priorities
  • This is the same structural problem seen in Brazil (SPI vs ministries), E-JUST (bilateral vs centralized), and Kumbh Mela (Mela Officer vs line departments)

Synthesis: Applying Case Lessons

Brazil

Build accountability through information systems, not hierarchy. Stanton should create a shared MIS for the Blackstone pilot — making progress visible across divisions. Use conditional resource mechanisms: tie additional project resources (budget, staffing, equipment) to demonstrated cross-media collaboration. Don't try to command; create structural incentives that make cooperation the rational choice.

Kumbh Mela

Distinguish directing from programming. Stanton needs to be clear about which role she's playing and when. Setting the pilot's strategic direction (directing) requires different engagement than designing the operational workflow (programming). Use pilot-as-test-cycle: treat Blackstone as an embodiment design — a structured experiment that generates feedback for future detailed design. Timely resolution matters: set clear milestones tied to meaningful deadlines, not open-ended "when we get to it" timelines.

E-JUST

Specify governance and management BEFORE launch. Don't repeat E-JUST's mistake of leaving governance vague and hoping goodwill fills the gaps. Don't let one person be the bottleneck: design decision-making protocols that distribute authority appropriately. Design collaboration protocols explicitly: spell out how cross-media teams will work together, resolve conflicts, and make joint decisions. Don't assume shared goals produce shared action.

Three Meta-Lessons

Meta-Lesson 1
Accountability Without Authority

You don't need command to get compliance. Information transparency + conditional resources can substitute for hierarchical control. Make performance visible; let social and political dynamics do the enforcement.

Meta-Lesson 2
Design Before Doing

Distinguish between conceptual, embodiment, and detailed design stages. Separate directing from programming roles. Use pilots as structured experiments with feedback loops, not just smaller versions of the full program.

Meta-Lesson 3
Governance Is Infrastructure

Governance structures are not overhead — they are load-bearing infrastructure. Specify them explicitly, distribute decision-making, and design collaboration protocols before launch. Vagueness on governance is not flexibility; it's a structural defect.

Framework Analysis

Where Herbert Simon Falls Short

Three gaps in Simon's decision-making framework exposed by applying it to the Blackstone case.

1
Management Work Is Not Just Decision-Making

Simon's framework treats management as decision-making: identifying alternatives, evaluating consequences, choosing. But the Kumbh Mela case reveals that management also involves directing (setting intentions, resolving conflicts, maintaining commitment) and programming (translating intentions into operational sequences). These are distinct activities that don't reduce to "choosing among alternatives." Stanton doesn't just need to make decisions — she needs to direct a cross-media effort and program operational workflows for regional staff.

2
Design Stages Matter

Simon distinguishes "programmed" from "non-programmed" decisions but doesn't account for the design process through which complex solutions evolve. Pahl & Beitz show that design moves through conceptual, embodiment, and detailed stages — each with different requirements, constraints, and types of decisions. Stanton's Blackstone pilot isn't a single decision; it's a design process that needs to move through these stages deliberately. Confusing embodiment-level questions with detailed-design questions (or vice versa) leads to dysfunction.

3
Time and External Constraints

Simon's framework is relatively silent on timely resolution — the reality that many management situations have hard deadlines driven by external factors (religious calendars for Kumbh Mela, political cycles for Brazil in Action, student arrival dates for E-JUST). Time doesn't just constrain the search for alternatives; it fundamentally shapes what kind of management work is possible. The Blackstone pilot must operate within political windows — if it takes too long to show results, support evaporates. Timely resolution is a management imperative, not just a practical constraint.

These three gaps don't invalidate Simon — they extend him. Simon gives us the analytical starting point (bounded rationality, satisficing, programmed vs. non-programmed decisions). But for public management practice, we need the additional lenses of directing/programming (from Fayol + Kumbh Mela analysis), design stages (from Pahl & Beitz), and timely resolution (from the cases themselves) to fully understand what Stanton must do.
