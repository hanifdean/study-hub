Before Brazil in Action, Cardoso's agenda was seen as only about macroeconomic stability ‚Äî the Real Plan (1994) that tamed hyperinflation.

üìö Historical Inspiration: Kubitschek's Plano de Metas

Juscelino Kubitschek (President 1956-1961) built Bras√≠lia ‚Äî Brazil's new capital, constructed from scratch in the interior.

What was Bras√≠lia? ‚ñ∂

Brazil's current capital city. Before 1960, the capital was Rio de Janeiro (on the coast). Kubitschek built Bras√≠lia as a massive, symbolic project to signal modernization and develop the interior. It was the capstone of many infrastructure projects.

What was the Plano de Metas approach? ‚ñ∂
  • Bundle many projects under ONE catchy name
  • Make all projects feel like a unified national effort
  • President personally associated with visible progress (visiting sites)
  • Simple message the public could rally behind
A shop owner showed his notebook with "Plano de Metas" written on the cover: "The president made a Plano de Metas for Brazil, and I made a plano de metas for my shop."
Why couldn't Cardoso copy Kubitschek's method? ‚ñ∂

Kubitschek bypassed the regular ministries by creating parallel agencies ("indirect administration") that reported directly to him.

But by 1988, this model was discredited ‚Äî the Constitution now favored "direct administration" (working through regular ministries).

Cardoso also had a well-publicized "managerial reform of the state" agenda ‚Äî bypassing bureaucracy would contradict this.

The Solution

Influence through information rather than control through hierarchy. Use transparency (MIS) + accountability + presidential backing as substitutes for formal authority.

üè¶ The "Inverted Priorities" Innovation

Problem: Even with ring-fenced money, ministries could still spend Brazil in Action funds on other things they cared about more.

Asti's insight (from BNDES banking):

"When I finance a company building a steel plant, I pace the release of resources in tune with the project's execution. If the project is not going well, I stop the flow of credit."
‚Äî Aluysio Asti

New procedure:

  1. Treasury does NOT release funds automatically
  2. Ministries must formally REQUEST funds from Planning
  3. Planning only approves if satisfied with progress
  4. No progress = no money
"This banality changed everything."
‚Äî Asti
üíª Management Information System (MIS)
  • Web-based (novel in 1996!)
  • Each project = one webpage: goals, investment, physical & financial programming
  • Built in 40 days by ~10 people
  • New versions every 8 months based on feedback
  • Added windows for: near-term problems + measures to avert them
  • President accessed 1-2x per week
"Transparency by itself would begin to induce managers to take more responsibility, insofar as it would be possible for people to follow what was happening with more clarity. Poor results would reflect badly on managers."

üìñ Key Concepts

Turf Battles
Conflicts between agencies when one encroaches on another's domain, authority, or resources. Avoided by keeping managers in sectoral ministries.
Inverted Priorities
When ministries spend earmarked funds on their own priorities instead of presidential priorities. Solved by conditional fund release.
Direct vs. Indirect Administration
Direct = through regular ministries. Indirect = parallel agencies. 1988 Constitution favored direct administration.
Ring-fencing
Protecting certain funds from budget cuts or reallocation. Necessary but not sufficient without conditional release.
PPA (Plano Pluri-annual)
Four-year investment/expenditure plan required by Brazil's Constitution. Brazil in Action projects were selected from this.
Transparency as Control
Using visible information (MIS) to create accountability without formal hierarchical authority.

üéì Lessons for Public Management

1Ô∏è‚É£ Resources vs. Management
"The lack of results in the public sector, in large measure, does not stem from the lack of resources, but rather from the lack of management."
‚Äî Senior Planning Ministry official

Brazil in Action proved that with proper management systems, existing resources could deliver results that previously seemed impossible.

2Ô∏è‚É£ Transparency as Substitute for Hierarchy

When you lack formal authority, make information visible. The MIS created accountability without command-and-control ‚Äî poor performance was exposed, good performance was recognized.

3Ô∏è‚É£ Design Around Constraints

Cardoso couldn't bypass bureaucracy (constitutional/political constraints). Instead of fighting this, the team designed a system that worked with existing structures while changing incentives.

4Ô∏è‚É£ Behavioral Change Through Modeling

Training lectures weren't enough. The Planning secretariat created culture change by modeling the behavior they wanted ‚Äî Silveira embodied the entrepreneurial manager role.

5Ô∏è‚É£ Political & Administrative Integration

Brazil in Action combined:

  • Political: Presidential visibility, branding, media management
  • Administrative: Project management, MIS, conditional fund release

Neither alone would have succeeded. Political will without management systems = announcements without delivery. Management systems without political backing = no leverage over ministries.

üîë The Big Insight

The system didn't eliminate problems ‚Äî it made them visible and solvable. That's the essence of good management design: not preventing all failures, but creating the conditions for rapid detection and correction.
