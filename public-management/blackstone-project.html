1. Case Overview

In 1989, the Massachusetts Department of Environmental Protection (DEP) launched a pilot "cross-media" inspection program called the Blackstone Project. The goal: fundamentally change how the state conducted environmental inspections — shifting from medium-specific, compliance-oriented inspections to whole-facility, cross-media inspections oriented toward pollution prevention at the source.

The case ends at the decision point: Assistant Commissioner Pat Stanton must design the operational plan to make this work. The unresolved question IS the case.

2. The Core Problem: The Pollution Shell Game

Environmental regulation was organized in silos — air, water, and hazardous waste inspected separately by different inspectors under different federal laws. They didn't share reports, had different file systems, and sat next to each other but "knew very, very little about what the other program did."

This created the "pollution shell game":

1
Air
Smokestack scrubber removes air pollution using water
2
Water
Scrubber creates contaminated water
3
Waste
Treating water produces hazardous sludge
4
Land
Sludge goes to landfill

Pollution wasn't being reduced — just shuffled between media. Each program declared compliance within its silo while the total pollution remained the same.

"We had done the 80 percent that was easy. The last 20 percent was very expensive."
— Patricia Deese Stanton, on why end-of-pipe control had hit diminishing returns
3. The Innovation

Nikki Roy proposed combining two ideas:

🔍 Cross-Media Inspections

One team examines air, water, AND waste at a facility in a single visit — seeing the whole picture instead of isolated slices. This catches media transfers that single-medium inspections miss.

🌱 Source Reduction

Instead of asking "how do you treat this waste?" → ask "do you need to create this waste at all?" Shift enforcement actions toward preventing pollution at its origin, not controlling it after creation.

🤝 DEP-OTA Partnership

An unprecedented alliance between the regulator (DEP / "bad cop") and the technical assistance provider (OTA / "good cop"). DEP inspectors refer non-compliant companies to OTA for free help in source reduction, instead of just punishing them.

4. Overhauling DEQE → DEP

DEQE (Department of Environmental Quality Engineering) was Massachusetts' original environmental regulatory agency. It had a terrible reputation — "a dirty word" among regulated companies. The Tsongas Commission found it lacked a "clear sense of mission" and was too reactive.

In September 1988, new Commissioner Daniel Greenbaum restructured everything:

New BureauFocusMetaphor
Bureau of Waste Site CleanupCleaning up existing contamination"Past sins"
Bureau of Waste PreventionStopping ongoing pollution"Present sins"
Bureau of Resource ProtectionPreventing future damage"Future sins"

The reorganization created the institutional home (Bureau of Waste Prevention) and the leadership position (Stanton as Assistant Commissioner) that made the Blackstone pilot possible.

5. The Blackstone Project Is Born
Roy's Struggle (1987-1989)

Roy had been pushing since her March 1987 memo. She formed a Source Reduction Policy Advisory Committee, got endorsement — then the pilot was put "on hold for a year." Her first EPA grant application was rejected. Eventually DEM secured a $289K EPA grant, and DEP added positions via the National Governors Association.

Total resources: 4 new staff positions. That's all.

Site Selection

Only 1 of 4 regional directors (Barry Fogel in Worcester) was willing to host. The Blackstone River area was chosen: heavy concentration of electroplating, metal finishing, and electronics manufacturing polluting into Narragansett Bay. Good mix of large and small companies.

6. Seven Implementation Challenges
1. Silo Identity — Inspectors identified deeply with their program. They "grew to like and become more expert in" their area and didn't want to change. Programs didn't share reports, had different file systems and inspection styles.
2. Expertise Concerns — Tom Cusson argued it takes a full year to train an air inspector. "Two file drawers full of laws." His medical analogy: "You need heart surgery, you go to a heart specialist. The environmental area is pretty much the same kind of thing."
3. Quality Accountability — Section chiefs were responsible for inspection reports but reports would now come from inspectors outside their program. "I'm on the hook for a report written by someone who doesn't know my regulations."
4. Culture Clash — Hazardous waste = black and white ("it's there or not"). Air quality = more discretion. Wastewater = gray areas. These weren't just procedural differences — they were philosophical differences about what enforcement means.
5. EPA Bean-Counting — EPA grants required specific inspection numbers per medium. Cross-media visits didn't fit the counting system. 25% of DEP's budget (55% of air quality) depended on satisfying these requirements.
6. DEP-OTA Trust — OTA built industry trust through confidentiality (legally protected by TURA). Partnering with the enforcer threatened to destroy that trust. See Interagency Relations section below.
7. Impact on Firms — Four inspectors at a small electroplating shop = terrifying. Four inspectors at Wyman-Gordon (2,500 employees) = manageable but disrupts established relationships. See Relations with Firms section below.
7. Interagency Relations: The Snitch Problem
🎯 The Core Dilemma

OTA could only help companies if companies trusted OTA wouldn't report to DEP. But partnering with DEP threatened that trust. TURA law explicitly banned OTA from sharing information with DEP.

"We had spent a lot of time building trust among firms who thought, 'The only reason government comes to our plants is to inspect us and fine us.' And we said, 'No, we're here to provide technical assistance. We do not report anything to DEP.'"
— Tim Greiner, OTA
"DEP could really muff it up if we interacted with them. They could ruin the trust we built with industry."
— Tim Greiner, OTA

The real incidents: DEP inspectors would visit a site recently visited by OTA and find serious violations. The company would say: "Someone from the state was already here and they didn't say anything about this." This created tension on both sides — DEP felt OTA was protecting violators; companies suspected OTA was tipping off DEP.

Greenbaum called it the "good guy-bad guy, white hat-black hat syndrome."

9. Stanton's Task
⚡ The Central Tension

Stanton has the responsibility but not the authority. She's the HQ official with direct responsibility for air quality, industrial wastewater, and hazardous waste programs. But she does NOT have direct line authority over the regional offices and inspectors who will execute the pilot. Regional offices report to the Deputy Commissioner for Operations — a completely different chain of command.

Her background: 2.5 years as director of DEP's Division of Water Supply; previously a project manager at Arthur D. Little consulting — experience that would serve her well. She beat Barry Fogel (who's hosting the pilot) for the assistant commissioner job.

She must design an operational plan that:

  • Gets section chiefs on board despite their resistance
  • Structures cross-media teams despite the expertise gap
  • Satisfies EPA's program-specific requirements
  • Makes the DEP-OTA partnership work without destroying trust
  • Doesn't overwhelm small businesses
  • All without direct line authority
10. Framework: Herbert Simon's Design Cycle

From Chapter 3 of Public Management: Recovering Designs through Case Study Research

The Design Cycle Applied to Blackstone

The Blackstone Project IS an endeavor to develop an innovative system. Simon's design cycle maps onto it:

Analysis
Roy identifying media transfers, regulatory gaps
Synthesis
Imagining cross-media + OTA partnership
Testing
The pilot itself
Evaluation
Did it catch transfers? Did referrals work?
Decision
Adopt statewide, modify, or abandon?

Satisficing principle: Stanton doesn't need the perfect system — she needs one good enough to pilot, learn from, and iterate.

Where Simon Falls Short
Gap 1: Management ≠ Just Decision-Making

Simon only includes management at the decision-making stage. But Blackstone's hardest challenges are directing (mobilizing section chiefs, regional offices, OTA) and programming (designing operational protocols). These require authority, persuasion, and coordination that Simon doesn't address. Fix: Fayol/POSDCORB adds directing + programming as management tasks within the design cycle.

Gap 2: Design Stages Matter

The conceptual design is done (cross-media + source reduction + OTA). Stanton's job is embodiment and detailed design — translating the concept into specific team structures, reporting flows, and referral protocols. That's a different kind of work from creative synthesis. Fix: Pahl & Beitz distinguish conceptual → embodiment → detailed design.

Gap 3: Time Constraints Are Specific

Simon says "search until good enough or time runs out." But Blackstone has EPA grant periods, training timelines, and political windows. Stanton needs timely resolution — a workable plan early enough that implementation can happen. Fix: work backwards from deadlines, not just forward from ideas.

11. Framework: Robert Simons' Diagnostic Controls

A diagnostic control system needs: (1) measurable outputs, (2) predetermined standards, (3) correction mechanisms.

Three Layers of Diagnostic Control for Blackstone
Layer 1: Satisfy Existing EPA System

EPA counts inspections by medium. Don't fight these metrics — translate into them. Each cross-media visit must be documented to count as separate air, water, AND hazwaste inspections. Create a dual reporting template: one cross-media report internally, plus medium-specific extracts for EPA. If program counts fall behind, supplement with traditional inspections.

Layer 2: Build New Cross-Media Metrics

Track what matters for the pilot: media transfers identified, OTA referrals made, source reduction plans adopted, time-to-inspection, cross-training hours. Set modest Year 1 targets to build credibility. Monthly review meetings between Stanton, Fogel, and Maher.

Layer 3: Monitor the Interagency Relationship

Track DEP-OTA referral volume, OTA response time, company willingness to work with OTA, and trust incidents (confidentiality breaches or companies complaining). Zero tolerance on confidentiality breaches. Quarterly joint DEP-OTA reviews.

⚡ Why this solves Stanton's authority problem: She can't order section chiefs to comply — but she CAN define what gets measured. Whoever controls the metrics controls behavior. By making pilot performance visible to the Commissioner and legislature, everyone has incentive to hit targets regardless of reporting lines. Diagnostic controls create authority through systems, not org charts.
12. Cross-Case Synthesis

How do lessons from Brazil in Action, the Kumbh Mela, and E-JUST apply to Blackstone?

🇧🇷 Brazil in Action → Authority Without Hierarchy

The parallel: SPI had ~30 staff, no formal authority over ministries — identical to Stanton's position.

Lesson 1: Build accountability through information. Brazil created the MIS making performance visible. Stanton should create a transparent monitoring system for the pilot.

Lesson 2: Use conditional resource mechanisms. Brazil tied fund release to milestones. Stanton controls 4 new staff positions — allocate them conditionally based on pilot progress.

Lesson 3: Invest in relationships, not directives. SPI succeeded by becoming a trusted partner. Stanton should work alongside Fogel and Maher, not issue plans from HQ.

Warning: SPI worked because it had presidential backing. Stanton needs Commissioner Greenbaum to visibly signal this matters.

🇮🇳 Kumbh Mela → Design Through Test-Iterate Cycles

The parallel: Both are developing innovative systems that don't exist yet under time pressure.

Lesson 1: Separate directing from programming. Ashish Goel directed; Saloni Goel programmed. Stanton should direct (mobilize resources, clear obstacles) while delegating programming (operational details) to someone in the regional office.

Lesson 2: Use the pilot as a design test. The Magh Mela was a real-world test before the full Kumbh. Blackstone is embodiment design + testing — expect iteration. The first cross-media inspection will be messy. That's the point.

Lesson 3: Timely resolution. Launch imperfectly early rather than perfectly late. Start with a few willing companies, learn, refine, expand.

🇪🇬🇯🇵 E-JUST → Specify Governance Before Launch

The parallel: E-JUST's R/D was detailed on academics but vague on governance — the Blackstone idea is well-articulated but operational management arrangements are underspecified.

Lesson 1: Don't repeat the governance gap. E-JUST launched without standard operating procedures for management → soft opening off-track. Stanton must specify management arrangements before launch.

Lesson 2: Don't let one person become a bottleneck. Khairy's centralized style paralyzed E-JUST. Stanton must delegate operational authority to Fogel and Maher while retaining strategic oversight.

Lesson 3: Design the collaboration explicitly. E-JUST assumed the Egyptian-Japanese partnership would work itself out. It didn't. The DEP-OTA modus operandi must be explicitly designed — when to refer, what's shared, what happens when violations are found.

Three Meta-Lessons for Blackstone
LessonSourceApplication
Authority comes from systems, not org charts Brazil in Action Build monitoring and resource mechanisms that create accountability without hierarchical authority

💡 The Integrating Insight

Every case shows that the gap between policy vision and operational reality is where endeavors succeed or fail. Brazil bridged it with MIS + conditional funding. Kumbh Mela bridged it with management tasks coupled to technical tasks. E-JUST failed to bridge it because governance was underspecified. Stanton's operational plan IS the bridge — and it needs to address authority, design process, and management specification simultaneously.
