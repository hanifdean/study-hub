Academic Disciplines About

üîë Core Argument: Need for a "Science of the Artificial" ‚ñ∂
Natural sciences study what is ‚Äî phenomena that exist independently of human design
Many disciplines actually study what is designed ‚Äî man-made artifacts, systems, and institutions
These disciplines are inherently normative (concerned with how things ought to be) rather than purely descriptive
Simon argues we need distinct "sciences of the artificial" ‚Äî not just natural science methods applied to artificial things
üèõÔ∏è The Professional Schools as Sciences of the Artificial ‚ñ∂
Engineering ‚Äî designs machines, structures, processes
Medicine ‚Äî designs interventions & therapies for health
Business & Management ‚Äî designs organizations, strategies, markets
Architecture ‚Äî designs buildings & spaces for human use
Education ‚Äî designs curricula, learning experiences
Law ‚Äî designs legal systems & institutional rules
Computer Science ‚Äî designs programs, algorithms, computational systems
Public Administration ‚Äî designs policies, governance structures
All share a common concern: design ‚Äî creating artifacts that achieve goals in given environments
‚ö†Ô∏è The Crisis of Identity ‚ñ∂
Professional schools tried to gain academic respectability by adopting natural science methodology
This led to a loss of distinctive identity ‚Äî teaching analysis over synthesis, description over design
"Engineers became physicists, business schools became economics departments"
Design curriculum was squeezed out in favor of analytical, descriptive courses
Result: graduates skilled in analysis but poorly trained in the art of design
üéØ Natural vs. Artificial: Key Distinctions ‚ñ∂
Natural things: shaped by natural laws alone ‚Äî rocks, weather, organisms (qua biological systems)
Artificial things: designed by humans to serve purposes and goals ‚Äî they are shaped to fit their environment
Artificial things are adapted to goals ‚Äî they can be evaluated by how well they serve intended functions
Artificial things involve imperatives (ought) alongside indicatives (is)
Key: artificial things are an interface between inner environment (structure) and outer environment (context)
üîÑ Implications for Public Management ‚ñ∂
Public organizations are artificial artifacts ‚Äî designed systems, not natural phenomena
Studying them requires understanding design intent, not just behavioral patterns
Policy-making is fundamentally a design activity ‚Äî crafting solutions to achieve goals in complex environments
Public management should reclaim design thinking as core to the discipline, not just analytical methods
üí° Key Takeaway: Simon argues that many academic disciplines (especially professional schools) study artificial phenomena but have lost their design-oriented identity by imitating natural sciences. A proper "science of the artificial" would restore design as the central intellectual activity.
Representing
Particular Machines
üîë The Artifact as Interface ‚ñ∂
Every artifact sits at the meeting point of two environments
Inner environment ‚Äî the substance, structure, and organization of the artifact itself
Outer environment ‚Äî the surroundings in which the artifact operates and must achieve its purposes
The artifact is the interface between these two ‚Äî it mediates between inner structure and outer demands
"If the inner environment is appropriate to the outer environment, the artifact will serve its intended purpose"
üîÄ Functional Equivalence (Multiple Realization) ‚ñ∂
Many different inner environments can satisfy the same outer environment requirements
Example: a clock can use mechanical gears, quartz crystal, or atomic oscillation ‚Äî all tell time
Example: a building can be made of wood, stone, or steel ‚Äî all provide shelter
This means the function of an artifact can be described independently of its specific structure
Implication: we can study what a machine does without knowing exactly how it does it internally
üìã Two Modes of Description ‚ñ∂
State description ‚Äî describes the properties of the world (what it looks like at a point in time)
Blueprints, photographs, structural diagrams
Answers: "What IS this thing?"
Process description ‚Äî describes the actions needed to produce or operate something
Recipes, algorithms, instructions, programs
Answers: "How do you MAKE or RUN this thing?"
Both are needed: state descriptions tell us the goal; process descriptions tell us the path
A key challenge is translating between them ‚Äî going from "what we want" to "how to achieve it"
üñ•Ô∏è Simulation as Representation ‚ñ∂
Simulation = creating a simplified model that mimics the behavior of a system
We can represent a machine by simulating its behavior without replicating its exact physical structure
A computer can simulate a weather system, a factory, an economy ‚Äî all are artificial representations
Simulation bridges state and process descriptions: we define initial states, apply rules, observe outcomes
Key insight: simulation is a third mode of understanding ‚Äî neither pure theory nor pure experiment
üìê Hierarchy & Near-Decomposability ‚ñ∂
Complex artifacts can be described as hierarchies of subsystems
Near-decomposability: subsystems interact strongly within themselves but weakly with each other
This allows us to study each level independently ‚Äî simplifying representation of complex machines
Example: a car ‚Üí engine system, electrical system, braking system ‚Äî each analysable on its own
Enables practical design: solve sub-problems separately, then integrate at the interface
üîÑ Implications for Public Management ‚ñ∂
Public organizations are complex machines ‚Äî representable through inner/outer environment analysis
Policy outcomes depend on the fit between organizational design (inner) and societal needs (outer)
Functional equivalence: many different organizational structures can achieve the same policy goals
Near-decomposability helps manage complexity: departments/agencies as semi-independent subsystems
Simulation and modeling can help test policy designs before implementation
üí° Key Takeaway: Simon shows that any particular machine (artifact) can be understood through its inner/outer environment interface, described via state or process descriptions, simulated, and decomposed hierarchically. These tools of representation apply equally to physical machines and to organizational/policy systems.
