1. Social Utility vs Social Intent

Social utility is a positive side-effect of a profit-seeking firm (e.g., Facebook reconnecting families), whereas social intent means the social good is the core purpose and financial returns are a by-product.

Dankbaar & Groot (2014) show this distinction in their Skype case: the tool created social value, but the primary intent remained commercial.

Mair & Mart√≠ (2006) note that in social enterprises, financial objectives are not primary; they are subordinated to mission.

Key takeaway

Social enterprises are defined by intent, not just outcomes. Utility can be accidental; intent is designed and operationalized.

4. Institutional Voids

Douglass North (1990) distinguishes between formal institutions (laws, courts, financial systems) and informal institutions (culture, ethics, norms).

Khanna & Palepu coined ‚Äúinstitutional voids‚Äù to describe missing or weak institutions in emerging markets.

These voids make markets more expensive (incomplete info, difficulty evaluating partners, weak contract enforcement) and more risky.

Formal institutional voids

Weak legal systems, unreliable courts, missing credit bureaus, inadequate infrastructure.

Informal institutional voids

Low trust, opaque norms, ethics gaps, weak civic culture or enforcement of social contracts.

5. Social Intrapreneurship vs Social Entrepreneurship

Social intrapreneurship happens inside corporations, NGOs, or public agencies: innovators launch initiatives that do well and do good while leveraging existing resources.

Social entrepreneurship is a standalone startup whose core purpose is social good delivered through a financially sustainable model.

Reference: Neessen, Cani√´ls, Vos & de Jong (2018) propose an integrated model of intrapreneurship that blends personal initiative with organizational support.

7. Hybridity Theory ‚Äî The Core Challenge

Social enterprises are hybrids mixing charity and business ‚Äî historically considered distinct and incompatible.

Continuum: Charity/Not-for-Profit ‚Üê Hybrid (SE) ‚Üí Commercial Entrepreneurship.

DimensionPro-ProfitPro-Social
Strategic IntentMaximize shareholder valueMaximize social impact
Critical TasksMarket growth, profitabilityMission delivery, community reach
CompetenciesCommercial execution, scaleSocial program design, empathy
Controls & RewardsFinancial metrics, incentivesImpact metrics, mission alignment
Overspecialising risks

Too social: mission drift, lopsided resource development, inability to capitalise on assets.

Too financial: lack of investment in measurement, neglect of social need, identity tensions.

Core insight

Social entrepreneurship is essentially about the management of these trade-offs.

8. Scaling Challenges

Seven key inhibitors (plus additional operational frictions) constrain scaling ‚Äî these are the focus of the rest of the course.

Affordability (Customer)

Low ability to pay limits revenue and reach.

Attracting Finance (Financial)

Capital is scarce, expensive, or misaligned with impact goals.

Ecosystem Complexity

Multiple stakeholders and dependencies slow execution.

Institutional Challenges

Formal & informal voids raise transaction costs.

Impact Measurement (Social Good)

Hard-to-quantify outcomes weaken accountability.

Value Proposition (PMF)

Solution-market fit is fragile in resource-poor contexts.

Managerial (Cognition)

Decision-making under ambiguity strains leadership.

Competitiveness (Strategic)

Competing incumbents can out-scale SEs.

Internal (Operational)

Systems, talent, and process limitations slow growth.
