Five cases exploring who bears the cost of social innovation ‚Äî and what happens when business models collide with social missions.

üíâ Case 1: Insulin

In 1923, Frederick Banting sold the insulin patent to the University of Toronto for $1, declaring: "Insulin does not belong to me, it belongs to the world." He believed a life-saving drug should be a public good.

A century later, the global insulin market is controlled by three companies ‚Äî Eli Lilly, Novo Nordisk, and Sanofi ‚Äî who together hold over 90% of market share. In the US, a vial of insulin that costs ~$5 to produce has been priced at $300+. One in four American diabetics rations their insulin due to cost.

üî¥ Core Tension

Public good origins ‚Üí private monopoly outcomes. The insulin case shows how a discovery explicitly intended to be free and universal was captured by market dynamics ‚Äî patents, regulatory moats, and pricing power ‚Äî over decades. It's been a century and insulin is still unaffordable for many.

Key Numbers

‚Ä¢ Patent sold for $1 (1923)

‚Ä¢ Production cost per vial: ~$5

‚Ä¢ US list price per vial: $300+ (before recent caps)

‚Ä¢ Market controlled by: 3 firms (Eli Lilly, Novo Nordisk, Sanofi)

‚Ä¢ 1 in 4 US diabetics ration insulin due to cost

üè¶ Case 2: Banco Compartamos

Compartamos began as an NGO providing microloans to low-income women in rural Mexico ‚Äî the kind of social enterprise Muhammad Yunus championed with Grameen Bank. Small loans, group lending, financial inclusion for the excluded.

Then it converted to a for-profit bank. Then it IPO'd in 2007. The IPO was wildly successful for investors ‚Äî but the mechanics of that success tell a darker story.

The Numbers That Disturbed Everyone

‚Ä¢ Annual interest rates on microloans: ~90%

‚Ä¢ Return on equity (ROE): ~40%

‚Ä¢ Borrowers: low-income women in rural Mexico

‚Ä¢ IPO enriched: founders, early investors, and VCs

‚ùå Mission Drift

To deliver 40% ROE to shareholders, Compartamos had to charge rates that trapped borrowers in cycles of debt. The very people the organization was created to serve became the source of profit extraction. Muhammad Yunus publicly condemned it as a betrayal of microfinance principles.

‚ö†Ô∏è The Cautionary Lesson

Compartamos is the textbook case of what happens when a social enterprise scales through for-profit capital without structural guardrails. When the revenue model requires extracting maximum value from your beneficiaries, profit and mission are fundamentally at odds.

üè• Case 3: OneDay Health

In rural Uganda, roughly 5 million people live more than 5km from the nearest health facility ‚Äî what OneDay Health calls healthcare "black holes." For these communities, even basic medical care (malaria testing, wound treatment, prenatal checkups) requires a half-day journey.

OneDay Health's model is radically simple: set up micro health centres embedded directly in rural communities, each staffed by a trained community health worker.

‚úÖ The Model

‚Ä¢ Setup cost per clinic: $4,000

‚Ä¢ Patient fee per visit: $1‚Äì2

‚Ä¢ Centres operating: 70+

‚Ä¢ Focus: local, asset-light, community-embedded

OneDay Health doesn't try to build hospitals or scale into a national health system. It operates within the constraints of its context ‚Äî low-resource, rural, and community-driven. Each centre is designed to be sustainable at the local level, not dependent on external funding to survive.

üî¨ Zahra Typology: Social Bricoleur

OneDay Health is the classic Social Bricoleur ‚Äî working with available resources to solve immediate, local problems. It doesn't try to change the system. It works within the system's gaps, one village at a time.

üì± Case 4: SwipeRx

SwipeRx (formerly mClinica) entered Indonesia's pharmacy market through education ‚Äî a free platform where pharmacists could access continuing education and professional community. This built trust and a massive user base before SwipeRx ever tried to monetize.

The platform then expanded into wholesale distribution, connecting pharmacies directly with manufacturers and cutting out middlemen. The economics work: pharmacies get cheaper drugs, SwipeRx takes a margin, and patients benefit from lower prices.

Scale & Reach

‚Ä¢ 120,000+ pharmacy professionals on platform

‚Ä¢ ~10,000 pharmacies using wholesale (~30% of Indonesia)

‚Ä¢ Entry strategy: education-first ‚Üí wholesale ‚Üí retail

‚Ä¢ Revenue model: structurally aligned with social mission

‚úÖ Profit & Mission Aligned

Every additional pharmacy on SwipeRx's wholesale platform = more revenue AND better drug access. The incentives compound in the same direction. This is the opposite of the Compartamos model ‚Äî here, scaling actually deepens social impact.

üè™ Apotek Inofarma ‚Äî Double Social Impact

SwipeRx's retail expansion through Apotek Inofarma targets underserved peri-urban and rural areas with pharmacies led by women pharmacists ‚Äî creating two layers of impact: healthcare access AND economic empowerment.

üáßüá∑ Case 5: ImpulsoGov

ImpulsoGov is a Brazilian non-profit building technology to improve public healthcare prevention. Their platform connects Electronic Medical Records (EMR) with national health targets, helping municipal governments track and improve preventive care metrics.

Scale & Model

‚Ä¢ 81 municipalities using the platform

‚Ä¢ ~1 million citizens impacted

‚Ä¢ Funding: donor-funded, non-profit

‚Ä¢ Goal: improve public health prevention, not just treatment

üåç Zahra Typology: Social Engineer

ImpulsoGov isn't filling a gap or building a parallel structure ‚Äî it's trying to change how the existing public health system works. By connecting data infrastructure to policy targets, it aims to shift Brazil's healthcare from reactive treatment to proactive prevention.

‚ö†Ô∏è The Challenge

Demand exceeds capacity. As a donor-funded non-profit, ImpulsoGov can't scale as fast as demand requires. More municipalities want to join than the organization can support. The funding model creates a ceiling that a for-profit structure wouldn't have ‚Äî but a for-profit structure might compromise the mission.

üí° Key Insights

üí° The Alignment Test

"Scaling works when the revenue model and social impact compound in the same direction. When they diverge ‚Äî that's when mission drift creeps in."

SwipeRx ‚úÖ ‚Äî every new pharmacy increases both revenue and drug access. Compartamos ‚ùå ‚Äî growth required extracting more from the people they were supposed to help. Insulin ‚ùå ‚Äî public good captured by private monopoly over time.

üß† Sasya's Insight

"Stability > scalability ‚Äî not every organisation needs to scale; some interventions should be localised."

OneDay Health works precisely because it doesn't try to become a national health system. It's stable, community-embedded, and replicable without centralization. The pressure to "scale or die" is a VC framework, not a universal truth for social impact.

‚ö†Ô∏è The Funding Trap

ImpulsoGov shows the flip side: donor funding preserves mission purity but creates a capacity ceiling. Compartamos shows what happens when you remove that ceiling with for-profit capital. There's no clean answer ‚Äî only trade-offs to manage deliberately.
