1. Systems of Exchange (Biggart & Delbridge, 2004)

The Core Argument

Traditional economics treats "the market" as the default‚Äîany other form of exchange is seen as an imperfection or friction. Biggart & Delbridge argue this is wrong.

The "market" is but one form of exchange arena found in society. We argue that there may be qualitatively distinct types of socially organized exchange that support substantively different orientations to economic action and, hence, culturally different trading arenas.
Biggart & Delbridge, 2004, p. 29

The Problem with Traditional Economics

Classical economics offers a "perfect market" as a hypothetical ideal, but this makes markets empirically empty:

Deviations from the ideal are conceptualized as imperfections and social relations between economic actors as friction.
Biggart & Delbridge, 2004, p. 29
Markets [now] became empirically empty conceptualizations of the forums in which exchange costlessly took place.
Demsetz (1982), cited in Biggart & Delbridge, 2004, p. 29

Three Approaches to Understanding Markets

1. Economic Approach (Traditional)

Markets as price-setting mechanisms. The "perfect market" assumes: sufficient firms, homogeneous commodities, independent actors, complete knowledge.

2. Markets as Social Structures

Granovetter's (1985) "embeddedness" concept‚Äîmarkets are constituted by concrete social relations, not just rational individuals.

The market is constituted by concrete social relations and is not simply a collection of rational individuals.
Biggart & Delbridge, 2004, p. 30
3. Markets as Cultural Arenas

Markets as constructed social worlds with shared meanings, values, and beliefs.

Categories of economic action are culturally variable and socially constructed.
DiMaggio (1994), cited in Biggart & Delbridge, 2004, p. 30

Why This Matters for Social Innovation

If you're designing a social enterprise, you need to understand what kind of exchange system you're operating in. Imposing market logic where solidarity governs (or vice versa) will fail.

2. Community Economies in the Global South (Hossein & Kinyanjui, 2022)

The Core Argument

Western development has dismissed informal economies as "primitive." This reading argues the opposite:

Our research acknowledges that indigenous business cooperatives thrive in spite of the unleveled playing field that people outside of the West endure.
Hossein & Kinyanjui, 2022, p. 223

The Colonial Legacy

The authors trace how Western epistemologies labeled Global South practices as inferior:

Imperialism, slavery, and colonialism are all part of the origins of capitalism; and racist colonizers extracted resources from the Global South with the sole purpose of enriching their own economies.
Hossein & Kinyanjui, 2022, p. 223
Even after slavery and colonization was outlawed for its immorality, modernists were still permitted to label the people of the Global South as inept, primitive, communal, and incapable of managing their own affairs.
Hossein & Kinyanjui, 2022, p. 223

The Binary Problem

For too long, the binary has been extreme versions of capitalism versus communism controlling the thinking on economics. Cedric Robinson (1983) in Black Marxism rejected this binary to some extent because both of these theories were very much rooted in European social forces that were fundamentally racist, Western, and white.
Hossein & Kinyanjui, 2022, p. 224
Structural Adjustment Programs (SAPs)

The intent of these political projects was "to pursue ultra-liberalized free-market orthodoxy, in which people do not benefit from welfare-enhancing programs, and to remake these places into neoliberal market regimes of privatization" (p. 224).

The Indonesia Case: Arisan

The reading highlights research by Ririn Haryani & Kelly Dombroski on arisan:

In a quiet way, the members in the Arisan system challenge the rational economic man by proposing a caring relational subject who operates collectively with others and not through self-interest.
Hossein & Kinyanjui, 2022, p. 233
Arisan users can be co-opted by political systems, as noted, but the authors reveal how some users were able to influence the Indonesian Financial Service Authority to issue new regulations on financial inclusion. This collective organizing and coming together enables Arisan members to lobby the government for financial inclusion, and this is a major piece of transformative work.
Hossein & Kinyanjui, 2022, p. 233

Critique of INGOs

Western aid monies are used to fund international non-governmental organizations (INGOs), such as CARE International, who are given millions of US dollars to assist in building up community and local cooperatives. INGOs know full well that money cooperatives and ROSCAs are very much embedded in the societies they want to help. Yet, they are paid subsidies to mimic local collectives that African people have been doing for centuries, and rebranding them as village savings loans (VSL) programs.
Hossein & Kinyanjui, 2022, p. 225-226
The INGO Problem

Foreign consultants profit from "teaching" what local women have done for centuries. The knowledge flows one way‚Äîfrom communities to INGOs‚Äîwithout crediting or compensating the originators.

The Feminist Economics Lens

The cases in this book are anchored in the feminist theories of diverse economies (DE) led by J. K. Gibson-Graham (1996, 2006) and the Community Economies Research Network (CERN). For three decades or more, feminist scholars have been writing about the existence of community economies.
Hossein & Kinyanjui, 2022, p. 226
Most people in the Global South have had only non-capitalist experiences of solidarity and reciprocity.
Hossein & Kinyanjui, 2022, p. 226

Implications for Social Innovation

1. Know Your Exchange System

Before designing a social enterprise, understand what exchange logic already exists. ROSCAs are communal systems in Biggart & Delbridge's framework‚Äîdriven by solidarity and embedded relations.

2. Value Indigenous Knowledge

Don't reinvent the wheel. Community economies have solved problems for centuries. Partner with and learn from local knowledge rather than extracting it.

3. Question "Scaling" Assumptions

Western scaling models assume growth = good. Community economies prioritize sustainability, equity, and social cohesion over scale.

6. Discussion Questions

For Class Discussion
  1. Using Biggart & Delbridge's framework, which quadrant does arisan belong to? Why?
  2. The readings critique Western development as paternalistic. Is there a role for external support in strengthening community economies? Where's the line?
  3. If community economies are so effective, why haven't they "scaled"? Is that even the right question?
  4. How might social enterprises learn from rather than replace existing community economies?
  5. The arisan case shows grassroots influence on OJK policy. What conditions enabled this? How can community voices be amplified elsewhere?
