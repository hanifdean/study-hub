üê± Robin's TLDR

Lecture 1 was WHY (copper can't keep up). This lecture is HOW ‚Äî how do you actually make optical chips, and why is it so cheap? The secret: you don't need billion-dollar cutting-edge factories. Silicon photonics reuses the same 20-year-old equipment that made your old Pentium 4. The "recipe" ‚Äî not the oven ‚Äî is the moat.

1) Materials Platform

SOI (Baseline)

Silicon-on-Insulator is the standard canvas for guiding light with tight bends and CMOS compatibility.

SiN (Low Loss)

Silicon Nitride delivers ultra-low loss, handles high power, and survives heat without cracking.

InP / TFLN (Light + Modulation)

InP adds lasers; TFLN enables ultra-fast modulation for 1.6T optical links.

Emerging Layers

Germanium, III-V hybrids, and polymer waveguides improve detection, bandwidth, and packaging.

üê± Simply explained: Think of building a highway for light. SOI (Silicon-On-Insulator) is your standard asphalt ‚Äî it works great and is the default. SiN (Silicon Nitride) is like a premium smooth road ‚Äî less friction (loss), handles heavy trucks (high power), and doesn't crack in heat. But silicon can't CREATE light ‚Äî it can only guide it. So you need InP (Indium Phosphide) bolted on as the headlights. TFLN is the sports car upgrade ‚Äî insanely fast modulation for the 1.6T era.

2) Legacy Lithography Advantage

Waveguides are big

Optical wavelengths (~1.55¬µm) mean critical dimensions stay 400‚Äì500nm ‚Äî far above 3nm logic nodes.

DUV wins

Legacy 65-130nm DUV lithography prints these features perfectly at a fraction of EUV cost.

Capex is gone

Fully depreciated tools turn revenue into profit and accelerate adoption.

üê± Simply explained: This is the single most important concept for investors. Light waves are physically BIGGER than electrons (400-500nm vs 3nm). You literally cannot shrink optical waveguides below a certain size ‚Äî physics won't allow it. So the $350M EUV machines ASML sells? Useless here. A $5M DUV machine from 2005 does the job perfectly. It's like discovering that a bicycle works better than a Ferrari for your specific route.

3) Design Ecosystem

MPW Access

Multi-Project Wafers let startups prototype without owning a fab ‚Äî the photonic version of fabless.

Co-design

Electronics and photonics are designed together (EPDA) to manage power, thermal, and yield.

Simulation Stack

FDTD + EM solvers validate optical behavior before tape-out.

üê± Simply explained: The EDA wars are fascinating. Synopsys bought Ansys (the physics simulation king) ‚Äî now they own the whole design-to-verify pipeline. Cadence plays it differently ‚Äî they own the "canvas" (Virtuoso) where every chip must be drawn regardless of what simulator you use. Both collect tolls. FlexCompute's Tidy3D is the disruptor ‚Äî it uses GPUs to do in minutes what used to take days.

4) Economic Moat

Zero Capex

Legacy tools are fully paid off ‚Äî every wafer run is almost pure margin.

Golden Yield

Two decades of process tuning means near-zero defects and stable reliability.

Recipe Trap

Process recipes, etches, and sidewall smoothness are tacit, hard-to-copy know-how.

üê± Simply explained: Three moats stacked on top of each other: (1) Zero capex ‚Äî equipment is fully depreciated, revenue = profit. (2) Golden yield ‚Äî 20 years of perfecting these processes means near-zero defects. (3) The "recipe" trap ‚Äî investors think "old equipment = easy to copy." Wrong. Light scatters if walls are rough by 1nm. The etching recipes are decades of tacit knowledge. China can buy the oven but not the recipe.

5) Legacy Fab Profiles

TSMC

Uses old 65nm for the optical chip, but sexy 3D packaging on top. Best of both worlds.

Tower Semiconductor

A 2002 factory in Newport Beach making chips for 800G AI data center modules. "Old factory, new gold."

GlobalFoundries

Bought IBM's old CPU lines, now the #1 silicon photonics foundry. Standard platform everyone uses.

Intel

The OG ‚Äî 8M+ photonic chips shipped, 32M lasers. Sold the transceiver biz to focus on the core tech.

Samsung

The dark horse. Not making the optical chips, but their packaging (I-Cube, X-Cube) could be the glue that puts it all together with HBM.

üê± Quick scorecard: TSMC (old node + advanced packaging), Tower Semi (old fab = new gold), GlobalFoundries (standard platform), Intel (shipping scale + lasers), Samsung (packaging wildcard).

6) Investment Insights

EDA toll collectors

Synopsys + Cadence extract steady rents as photonics design volume scales.

Test equipment as gatekeeper

Photonic testing and packaging are bottlenecks with stable pricing power.

Moat analysis matters

Foundries with mature recipes capture outsized margin ‚Äî copycats struggle on yield.

üìñ Jargon Decoder

CMOS (Complementary Metal-Oxide-Semiconductor) ‚Ü© back

The standard semiconductor manufacturing process used for most chips. Silicon photonics leverages existing CMOS fabs to reduce costs.

SOI (Silicon-on-Insulator) ‚Ü© back

A wafer structure with a thin silicon layer on top of an insulating oxide layer. Provides better optical waveguide properties for photonic circuits.

Waveguide ‚Ü© back

A structure that guides light signals along a defined path on a chip, similar to how copper traces guide electrical signals.

Modulator ‚Ü© back

A device that encodes data onto a light beam by varying its intensity, phase, or other properties. Converts electrical signals to optical.

Photodetector ‚Ü© back

A device that converts incoming light signals back into electrical signals. The "receiver" end of an optical link.

Transceiver ‚Ü© back

A module that both transmits and receives optical signals, converting between electrical and optical domains.
