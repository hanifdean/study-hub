üìå Worth Knowing

  • GPU allocation is geopolitical ‚Äî the US government now controls who gets access to advanced AI chips (H100, B200) through export controls. Countries like Saudi Arabia and UAE can only get GPUs with US approval. Nvidia has even designed "export-compliant" chips (H20) with reduced capabilities for China. Whoever controls GPU allocation controls the pace of AI development globally.
  • The CoreWeave phenomenon ‚Äî CoreWeave went from a small crypto mining company to a $35B+ GPU cloud provider in ~3 years. They secured massive Nvidia GPU allocations early, signed long-term contracts with Microsoft and AI labs, then went public. Their story illustrates the explosive demand for dedicated AI compute that hyperscalers couldn't satisfy fast enough.
  • The hyperscaler capex arms race ‚Äî Meta, Microsoft, Google, and Amazon are each spending $50-80B+ per year on data centers, mostly for AI. This is unprecedented ‚Äî it exceeds what was spent building the entire US interstate highway system (inflation-adjusted). The bet: AI will be as fundamental as electricity, and whoever has the most compute wins.
  • Why not just buy your own GPUs? ‚Äî Building and operating your own GPU cluster requires expertise in power, cooling, networking, and software. A single rack of 8 H100s costs ~$300K+ just for the hardware, plus massive ongoing costs. Cloud providers handle all of this complexity ‚Äî you just rent by the hour. Only the largest AI labs (OpenAI, Google, Meta) operate significant owned infrastructure.
  • The power problem ‚Äî AI data centers are incredibly power-hungry. A single Nvidia GB200 NVL72 rack draws ~120kW. Hyperscalers are now the largest buyers of nuclear power, natural gas, and renewable energy. Microsoft restarted Three Mile Island. Amazon bought a nuclear-powered data center campus. Power availability is becoming the real bottleneck, not GPU supply.
