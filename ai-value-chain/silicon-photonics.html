Silicon Photonics: The Next Fabless Revolution

When electrons hit the power wall, light takes over. Silicon photonics turns CMOS fabs into optical highways, enabling massive bandwidth inside chips and data centers - without building new fabs.

üê± Robin's TLDR ‚Äî What is this really about?

The one-liner: Data centers are drowning in heat because copper wires can't keep up. Silicon photonics replaces copper with light inside chips ‚Äî and the genius part is it reuses the same old factories that make regular chips.

Think of it like this: Imagine a highway system (copper wires) that was built for 1990s traffic. Now you need it to handle 1000x more cars. You can't just add more lanes ‚Äî the road itself melts from friction. Silicon photonics is like replacing the asphalt highway with a fiber-optic rail system ‚Äî same land, same route, but now cars are replaced by beams of light that don't create heat and don't crash into each other.

Why investors care: This isn't "future tech" anymore. AI data centers physically cannot scale further without this. And the companies that win aren't necessarily building new things ‚Äî they're the ones who can cheaply convert existing chip factories to make optical chips. That's the moat.

Power Wall Problem

Exponential data growth makes copper wiring bandwidth-limited and heat-constrained. The industry needs electrons for computation and photons for transmission ‚Äî now a necessity, not an option.

üê± Simply put

Electrons vs Photons = think of it as "balls vs ghosts." Electrons are like billiard balls ‚Äî they crash into each other, create friction, generate heat. The faster you push them through copper, the worse it gets. Photons (light particles) are like ghosts ‚Äî they pass right through each other. You can send millions of light signals through the same wire simultaneously (that's WDM), and they don't interfere or heat up. That's why fiber optic internet is so much faster than old copper DSL ‚Äî same principle, now being applied inside the chip itself.

Key Shifts (Tap to Expand)
Second Fabless Revolution: From Physics to Architecture

Optical design once demanded Maxwell-level physics expertise. Today, PDKs abstract the nanometer complexity and EPDA tools merge electronics + photonics. The center of gravity shifts to system architects who design scalable optical platforms.

üê± Simply put

Remember how in the 1980s, chip companies stopped needing their own factories? That was the first "fabless revolution" ‚Äî companies like Nvidia just designed chips and let TSMC build them. The same thing is now happening with optical chips. You used to need a PhD in physics to design anything with light on silicon. Now there are software tools (like Photoshop but for photonic circuits) that let engineers drag-and-drop optical components. The barrier to entry just collapsed.

Economic Moat: CMOS Infrastructure Reuse

Silicon photonics reuses existing CMOS fabs (Intel, TSMC, GlobalFoundries). Light wavelengths (1.31¬µm, 1.55¬µm) are far larger than electron paths, so legacy 65-130nm processes are ideal. Tower Semiconductor even leverages Intel's depreciated 65nm Fab 11X ‚Äî massive capital efficiency with zero new fab buildout.

üê± Simply put

This is the killer insight. Building a cutting-edge chip factory costs $20B+. But silicon photonics doesn't need cutting-edge. Light is physically bigger than electrons, so you can use "old" 65nm equipment that's already been paid off. It's like discovering your 15-year-old printer can print money. Tower Semiconductor literally uses Intel's old depreciated factory ‚Äî zero new capex, world-class optical chips. That's why this is investable now, not in 10 years.

Key Jargon Decoded

WDM (Wavelength Division Multiplexing) ‚Ü© back

Sending multiple colors of light through one fiber simultaneously. Each color = a separate data channel. Like having 80 radio stations on one antenna ‚Äî but with light.

CPO (Co-Packaged Optics) ‚Ü© back

Instead of plugging optical modules into a switch externally, you put the optics directly on the chip package. Less distance = less power = less heat. This is what Broadcom's Bailly platform does with Tomahawk switches.

PAM4 DSP ‚Ü© back

A way to encode 2 bits per symbol instead of 1 (so double the data rate). The DSP (Digital Signal Processor) cleans up the signal after it gets noisy traveling through fiber. Marvell's Inphi acquisition was all about this ‚Äî it's the "brain" that makes high-speed optical links actually work cleanly.

ELS (External Light Source) ‚Ü© back

Silicon is great at guiding light but terrible at generating it. So you need a separate laser (usually made from indium phosphide) to create the light beam. Coherent and Lumentum dominate this ‚Äî they're the "light suppliers" for the whole ecosystem.

PDK (Process Design Kit) ‚Ü© back

A toolkit from the foundry that tells designers "here are the building blocks you can use." Like a Lego instruction manual ‚Äî you don't need to know how plastic is made, just how to snap pieces together. This is what makes the fabless model possible.

Investment Insights

üê± Robin's framing for you

This maps directly to what you've been learning about the AI value chain. Silicon photonics sits at L5-L6 ‚Äî the networking/infrastructure layer. The same "picks and shovels" logic applies: don't just chase the chip designers, look at who controls the bottlenecks. In HBM, it was SK Hynix. In photonics, it's Coherent/Lumentum (they make the lasers everyone needs) and FormFactor (testing = the quality gatekeeper). And the CMOS reuse story is the clearest example of "capital efficiency as a moat" you'll find anywhere.

Follow the infrastructure transition

Value accrues to firms that efficiently adapt existing CMOS infrastructure to photonics - not just chip makers.

Laser light sources = bottleneck moats

Coherent and Lumentum hold quasi-monopoly positions for external light sources (ELS).

Picks & shovels stability

Testing and packaging players like FormFactor offer steadier revenue regardless of platform winners.

Disruptive small caps

POET (optical interposer), Aeluma (lasers on silicon), Ayar Labs (chip-to-chip optical).
