üìå Worth Knowing

  • Why Big Tech is going nuclear ‚Äî It's the only carbon-free energy source that provides 24/7 baseload power at the scale AI demands. Solar and wind are intermittent. Natural gas has emissions. Geothermal is geographically limited. Nuclear checks every box ‚Äî which is why companies that once avoided the word "nuclear" are now signing multi-billion-dollar deals.
  • The water controversy ‚Äî Data centers are competing directly with agriculture and local communities for water. In drought-prone areas like Phoenix and parts of Texas, this is becoming a major political issue. Some companies are shifting to air cooling or closed-loop systems, but these are less efficient and more expensive.
  • Stranded gas ‚Äî Some companies are building data centers directly next to natural gas wells in remote areas ‚Äî using "stranded" gas that would otherwise be flared (burned off as waste). This is controversial but pragmatic: it provides cheap power in locations with no grid constraints.
  • The transformer bottleneck ‚Äî Yes, "transformer" in this context means the electrical power transformer, not the AI architecture. These massive devices (some weigh 400+ tons) step down high-voltage transmission line power to usable levels. Only a handful of manufacturers exist worldwide, and the backlog is 2-3 years. This is one of the most underappreciated bottlenecks in AI infrastructure.
  • Energy costs = AI geography ‚Äî Wherever electricity is cheapest and most abundant, that's where AI compute will concentrate. This is why the Middle East (cheap natural gas), Nordics (cheap hydro), and nuclear-powered US sites are all racing to attract data center investment. Energy costs may ultimately determine where AI development happens ‚Äî and which countries lead.
