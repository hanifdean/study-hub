Optical Circuit Switching (OCS): Data Center Networks with Light

OCS reroutes traffic using pure light paths, eliminating costly optical-to-electrical conversions. It is the switching layer that makes massive GPU clusters and photonic interconnects scale.

üê± Robin's TLDR ‚Äî What is this really about?

The one-liner: OCS switches network traffic using actual light beams instead of converting light ‚Üí electricity ‚Üí light (O-E-O) at every hop.

Think of it like this: Traditional network switches are toll booths where every car must stop, show ID, and restart. OCS is like a highway interchange where roads physically move to redirect traffic ‚Äî no stopping needed.

Why it matters: Google already uses this (Project Apollo) and gets ~40% less power + 30% more throughput. This is the infrastructure layer that makes massive GPU clusters actually work.

OCS Definition & How It Works

OCS is Layer 1 switching that reroutes optical paths without O-E-O conversion. It is bandwidth-transparent (independent of baud rates), future-proof for 1.6T+ links, and measured by insertion loss (1.0‚Äì2.0 dB) plus switching speed.

Two dominant implementations: MEMS mirrors physically redirect light beams (Google Apollo, Lumentum) with millisecond switching; SiPh + SOA uses on-chip waveguides and SOA gain for nanosecond to microsecond switching (Salience Labs, iPronics).

üê± Simply put

Traditional switches are like post offices ‚Äî every package gets opened, read, re-packed, and forwarded. OCS is like a train track switch ‚Äî you physically redirect the whole track, and trains (data) flow through without stopping. Way less energy, way less delay.

üê± Simply put

Tiny mirrors on a chip that physically tilt to redirect light beams. Imagine a room full of tiny disco balls that you can precisely angle to bounce a laser beam to any destination.

Strategic Deployment Areas

OCS is most powerful in the spine layer (backbone interconnecting switches), cutting power/cost by >40%. It enables 3D torus topologies and real-time fault recovery inside TPU/GPU clusters. It also powers compute-to-memory fabrics (remote DRAM / CXL), ICI inside GPU trays, and DCI between data center buildings.

Memory Tiering & Resource Allocation

OCS enables DRAM pooling to optimize expensive HBM ratios via low-latency optical paths. With SDN control, physical topologies can be reconfigured in real-time per workload, making it a key component of Google‚Äôs next-gen architecture.

üê± Simply put

GPUs need lots of expensive HBM memory, but not all data needs to be in the fast lane. OCS lets you create a ‚Äúmemory highway‚Äù where servers can reach remote memory pools almost as fast as local memory. Key Jargon Decoded

OCS (Optical Circuit Switching) ‚Ü© back

Switching network traffic by reconfiguring light paths directly, without converting to electronics at every hop.

O-E-O (Optical-Electrical-Optical conversion) ‚Ü© back

The traditional step where light is converted to electricity for switching, then back to light ‚Äî a major power and latency hit.

MEMS (Micro-Electro-Mechanical Systems) ‚Ü© back

Tiny mechanical mirrors that physically steer light beams to new paths.

SOA (Semiconductor Optical Amplifier) ‚Ü© back

An on-chip amplifier that boosts optical signals and enables faster switching in photonic circuits.

SiPh (Silicon Photonics) ‚Ü© back

Using silicon to guide and process light for high-bandwidth optical interconnects.

SDN (Software-Defined Networking) ‚Ü© back

Centralized software control over network paths and topology.

FPPGA (Field Programmable Photonic Gate Array) ‚Ü© back

A reconfigurable photonic circuit, like an FPGA but for light paths.

CXL (Compute Express Link) ‚Ü© back

A protocol for shared memory and cache coherence across CPUs, GPUs, and accelerators.

DCI (Data Center Interconnect) ‚Ü© back

Long-haul connections between data center buildings or campuses.

ICI (Inter-Chip Interconnect) ‚Ü© back

The physical links that connect chips or chiplets inside a system.
