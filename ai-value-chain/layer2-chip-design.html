üìñ Jargon Decoder

Fabless ‚Ü© back

A semiconductor company that designs chips but outsources manufacturing to foundries like TSMC. Examples: Nvidia, AMD, Qualcomm.

IDM (Integrated Device Manufacturer) ‚Ü© back

A company that both designs and manufactures its own chips in-house. The traditional model, now rare. Examples: Intel, Samsung.

CUDA ‚Ü© back

Nvidia's proprietary parallel computing platform. The software ecosystem built over 20+ years that makes switching from Nvidia GPUs nearly impossible.

EDA (Electronic Design Automation) ‚Ü© back

Software tools used to design semiconductor chips. Synopsys and Cadence control ~70% of this market ‚Äî every advanced chip is designed using their tools.

ISA (Instruction Set Architecture) ‚Ü© back

The fundamental "language" that software uses to communicate with a chip (e.g., x86, ARM, RISC-V). Switching ISAs means rewriting/recompiling all software.

GDSII ‚Ü© back

The standard file format for the final chip design blueprint sent to the foundry for manufacturing. Contains all the geometric information for every layer.

TPU (Tensor Processing Unit) ‚Ü© back

Google's custom AI accelerator chip, optimized for TensorFlow/JAX workloads. Not sold externally ‚Äî only available via Google Cloud.

RISC-V ‚Ü© back

An open-source instruction set architecture with no license fees. Growing rapidly, especially in China and IoT applications.

üìå Worth Knowing

  • Why is CUDA so hard to unseat? ‚Äî It's not just software. It's 20+ years of libraries (cuDNN, TensorRT, NCCL), frameworks (PyTorch/TensorFlow optimized for CUDA), millions of trained developers, university courses, research papers, and corporate codebases. Switching away means retraining people, rewriting code, and accepting worse performance during the transition. The ecosystem is the moat, not the API.
  • Nvidia's software moat may be more valuable than its hardware. ‚Äî Hardware specs can be matched (AMD's MI300X competes on paper). But the software ecosystem is nearly impossible to replicate. Even if AMD's chips were 2√ó faster, most companies wouldn't switch because their entire stack is CUDA-dependent. Nvidia knows this ‚Äî it's why they invest billions in software R&D.
  • China's chip design capabilities are advancing rapidly. ‚Äî Huawei's Ascend 910C is a competitive AI chip designed entirely in China. Despite US export controls blocking advanced manufacturing equipment, Chinese companies are innovating around constraints ‚Äî using older process nodes more creatively, chiplet designs, and massive engineering teams. The design talent exists; the manufacturing bottleneck (TSMC) is the real constraint.
  • Why chip design costs have exploded. ‚Äî Designing at the leading edge (3nm, 2nm) requires thousands of engineers, billions in R&D, years of development, and $100M+ in EDA tool licenses alone. The mask set for a 3nm chip can cost $30M. This is why fewer and fewer companies can afford to design cutting-edge chips, and why Broadcom/Marvell's design services business is booming ‚Äî they amortize costs across multiple customers.
  • The AI chip startup graveyard. ‚Äî Dozens of startups (Graphcore, Cerebras, SambaNova, Habana) have tried to challenge Nvidia with novel architectures. Most have struggled or pivoted. The problem isn't building a good chip ‚Äî it's building the software ecosystem around it. Customers won't adopt a chip without robust software support, and developers won't build for a chip without customers. Classic chicken-and-egg problem that Nvidia solved over 20 years.
