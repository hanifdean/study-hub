The Taiwan Risk ‚Äî 100 Miles from Mainland China
Taiwan sits just 100 miles from mainland China. ~92% of the world's most advanced chip production is concentrated on this island. Any conflict, blockade, or even an earthquake could cripple the global technology supply chain. This is the single biggest geopolitical risk in technology today.

üìè Process Nodes Explained Simply

"nm" (nanometer) used to measure the size of transistor features. Smaller = more transistors packed in = more performance, less power consumption. But modern node names are mostly marketing ‚Äî "3nm" doesn't mean anything is actually 3 nanometers.

What matters is the transistor density ‚Äî how many transistors fit per square millimeter. Each new node packs roughly 1.5-2√ó more transistors.

‚ö†Ô∏è Why Node Names Are Mostly Marketing

In the 1990s, "nm" actually measured the gate length of a transistor. Today, nothing on a "3nm" chip is 3 nanometers. The number is a commercial label that roughly indicates the generation. Intel's "Intel 7" is roughly equivalent to TSMC's "5nm." Samsung's "3nm" has fewer transistors/mm¬≤ than TSMC's "3nm." The only thing that matters: transistor density, power efficiency, and performance.

üìñ Jargon Decoder

Foundry ‚Ü© back

A semiconductor fabrication facility that manufactures chips designed by other companies. TSMC is the world's largest pure-play foundry.

Process Node (nm) ‚Ü© back

A generation label for chip manufacturing technology (e.g., 3nm, 5nm). Smaller numbers generally mean more transistors, better performance, and lower power.

Yield Rate ‚Ü© back

The percentage of working chips produced from a wafer. Higher yield = lower cost per chip. TSMC's consistently high yields are a key competitive advantage.

Fab (Fabrication Plant) ‚Ü© back

A semiconductor manufacturing facility containing cleanrooms and hundreds of specialized machines. Costs $20B+ to build a leading-edge fab.

GAA (Gate-All-Around) ‚Ü© back

The next-generation transistor architecture replacing FinFET, where the gate wraps completely around the channel for better control at 2nm and below.

FinFET ‚Ü© back

The current dominant 3D transistor design where the gate wraps around a fin-shaped channel. Used from 22nm to 3nm nodes.

Cleanroom ‚Ü© back

An ultra-controlled environment with 10,000√ó fewer particles than outside air. Even a speck of dust can ruin a wafer with billions of nanometer-scale features.

Tape-out ‚Ü© back

The final step in chip design where the completed design is sent to the foundry for manufacturing. Named after the historical practice of sending design data on magnetic tape.

üìå Worth Knowing

  • Yield rates are everything ‚Äî Yield = the percentage of chips on a wafer that actually work. TSMC consistently achieves 80%+ yields on new nodes while Samsung struggles to break 50%. This means TSMC gets more working chips per wafer, making them cheaper and more profitable. It's TSMC's deepest competitive moat ‚Äî built over decades of institutional knowledge.
  • Why you can't just build fabs overnight ‚Äî A leading-edge fab takes 3-5 years from empty lot to first chips. It needs thousands of specialized engineers (process, equipment, yield, integration) who typically require 5-10 years of experience. You also need the entire ecosystem: chemical suppliers, gas suppliers, ultra-pure water systems, waste treatment ‚Äî all built to semiconductor-grade standards.
  • The water problem ‚Äî A single advanced fab uses 10+ million gallons of ultra-pure water per day. This water must be purified to a level where it has fewer than 1 part per billion of impurities. Taiwan has faced drought scares that threatened TSMC's production. TSMC now recycles 90%+ of its water.
  • TSMC's "silicon shield" theory ‚Äî Some strategists argue that Taiwan is "too important to invade" because China also depends on TSMC chips. If China attacked, the fabs would be destroyed or unusable, hurting China too. Critics counter that China might calculate the cost is worth it, or that as China builds domestic capacity, the shield weakens.
  • A single event could cripple global tech ‚Äî Taiwan sits on the Pacific Ring of Fire (earthquake zone). A magnitude 7.4 earthquake hit Taiwan in April 2024, temporarily halting TSMC production. A larger quake, a Chinese blockade, or even a prolonged power outage could disrupt chips for Nvidia, Apple, AMD, and every major tech company simultaneously.
