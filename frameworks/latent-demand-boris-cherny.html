Latent Demand & Building What People Already Do

Boris Cherny's single biggest product principle — and how Claude Code, Plan Mode, and Claude.md all emerged from watching existing behavior.

Boris Cherny (Creator of Claude Code) Light Cone Interview, Feb 2026 Product & Building
Core Principle

People Will Only Do a Thing They Already Do

Boris Cherny's definition of latent demand and why it's the single most important product principle he's ever learned.

People will only do a thing they already do. You can't get people to do a new thing. If people are trying to do a thing and you make it easier — that's a good idea.

— Boris Cherny, Creator of Claude Code
Latent Demand

People want something but aren't searching for it because they don't know a solution exists, can't articulate it, or have adapted to the bad status quo.

Expressed Demand

People actively Googling, comparing, requesting quotes. Easy to measure, but everyone's already competing for it. Crowded by definition.

No Demand

People genuinely don't want or need it. The graveyard of startups that confused "latent" with "nonexistent."

Nobody asked for Uber. But everyone was already trying to get from A to B and hating the taxi experience. The demand was latent — invisible in surveys, obvious in frustration. Your job isn't to create new behavior. It's to remove friction from existing behavior.

Why Latent Demand Beats New Behavior

Three reasons latent demand is where asymmetric opportunities live:

  • No competition signal: Expressed demand shows up in keyword tools, market reports, RFPs. Everyone sees it. Latent demand is invisible — you find it by watching what people hack together, not by reading reports.
  • Adoption is frictionless: You're not convincing anyone to change behavior. You're making their existing behavior effortless. The "aha" moment is instant.
  • Defensibility through insight: Finding latent demand requires taste and judgment (seeing what others don't). This is inherently harder to copy than following expressed demand signals.
Case Study

How Claude Code Emerged from Latent Demand

Every major Claude Code feature was built by watching what users were already doing — and making it native.

1
Claude Code Itself
Accidental product from a personal CLI

Boris built Claude Code as a personal CLI to test the API. He shared it with teammates. They started using it for real work. It went viral internally. The terminal form factor stuck because "we didn't know what the right UI was yet" — and it turned out the terminal was the right UI.

Latent Demand

Engineers were already opening terminals to write code. They were already talking to Claude in the browser. Boris just merged the two things they were already doing.

2
Plan Mode
People were already doing this in the browser

Before Plan Mode existed, users were opening Claude in the browser to talk through specs and architecture before coding in the terminal. Boris watched this behavior and moved the planning step into the CLI itself.

Boris on Plan Mode's Future

"Plan mode probably has a limited lifespan... maybe in a month the model figures it out on its own." He's already anticipating the model absorbing the feature — building for the model 6 months from now, not today.

3
Claude.md / CLAUDE.md
Users were already writing markdown files for themselves

People were writing markdown files with project context, style guides, and instructions — then manually having the model read them at the start of each session. Boris just formalized what users had hacked together.

4
Co-Work Mode
Non-technical users jumping through hoops

Non-technical users at Anthropic — finance, design, data science — were "jumping through hoops to install a terminal app." They wanted Claude Code's power but not the terminal interface. So Boris built a GUI wrapper. The demand was latent: people were already trying to do the thing, just badly.

In every case, the process was the same: watch what people hack together → formalize it → ship it natively. The product emerged from observing behavior, not from imagining features.

Frameworks for Builders

Three Rules from the Interview

Beyond latent demand, Boris articulated three principles that inform how he builds at Anthropic.

1
Build for the Model 6 Months from Now
The most counterintuitive product advice

At Anthropic, we don't build for the model of today. We build for the model 6 months from now. Just try to think about what frontier the model is not very good at today — it's going to get good at it.

— Boris Cherny

This means your product strategy should target the future capabilities of the model, not its current limitations. If something is hard for the model today, that's where you build — because the model will catch up, and your product will be ready.

2
The Bitter Lesson
Never bet against the model

Never bet against the model. You could build scaffolding to improve performance 10–20%... or just wait a few months and the model does it for free.

— Boris Cherny

This echoes Rich Sutton's "Bitter Lesson" from RL research: approaches that leverage computation always win over human-engineered cleverness. Build thin scaffolding, not thick frameworks.

Practical Implication

There's no part of Claude Code that was around 6 months ago. It's constantly rewritten. ~80% of the codebase is less than 2 months old. Scaffolding has a shelf life measured in months, not years.

3
Hiring Signal: Can You Be Wrong?
Judgment in action

I sometimes ask 'what's an example of when you were wrong?' — can they recognize mistakes, claim credit for them, and learn?

— Boris Cherny

This connects directly to the anti-portfolio concept from the Judgment Premium — the best performers track their errors. The ability to be wrong, own it, and extract signal from it is the meta-skill.

Connection: The Latent Demand Rule

Every new client or user interaction should start with ONE thing they already do. No pitch. Just: "What takes you 15 minutes every morning?" Deliver that. Then expand. The product should emerge from watching what people hack together.
